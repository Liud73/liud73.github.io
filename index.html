Track 1

Enhancing Community Resilience to Tree Damages from Ice Storms through Co‑design & Co‑learning

A CIVIC Innovation Challenge planning project in the Oklahoma City metro: partnering with community organizations to predict, prevent, and mitigate urban tree damage during freezing rain events.

Read abstract Project goals

Abstract

This project planning grant supports research on measures to predict and prevent urban tree damage caused by ice storms in the Southern Great Plains. Freezing rain can accumulate significant ice loads on tree canopies and built infrastructure. While the public is familiar with severe weather alerts, communities are less prepared for cascading risks from urban tree failures—damaging property, blocking roads, and disrupting utilities.

Partnering with industry, government, and non-profits in Oklahoma City (OKC), we combine community engagement and participatory research to empower residents in data collection, problem‑solving, and resilience‑building. Using the OKC metro as a pilot, we analyze factors contributing to urban tree vulnerability and co‑design interventions to mitigate risk.

Stage 1 activities include: (1) building a framework to identify areas at risk of ice‑induced tree damage; (2) co‑producing interventions that reflect community priorities; and (3) advancing education and awareness about urban forestry and ice‑storm risk. Outcomes include shareable tools and practices that strengthen preparedness and adaptation across regions and sectors.

Background

About the NSF Civic Innovation Challenge (CIVIC), the Stage 1 planning phase, and why this work matters for the OKC metro area.

What is the NSF CIVIC Challenge?

A National Science Foundation initiative that funds community‑driven innovation. It emphasizes partnerships between researchers and local stakeholders to solve real‑world problems.

What is Stage 1?

A planning and research phase (up to $75,000) to build partnerships, conduct pilot studies, and gather community input—laying the groundwork for Stage 2 implementation (up to $1M).

Our focus

Tree hazard monitoring and early warning during freezing rain events, integrating atmospheric science, urban forestry, and community reporting.

Why it matters
  • Ice storms cause tree failures, outages, and road hazards.
  • Resilience reduces damage, enhances safety, and protects the urban forest.
  • Community participation ensures tools are usable and accessible.

Research Purpose

We combine scientific modeling with lived experience to co‑design practical solutions that communities can deploy before, during, and after ice storms.

Understand community experiences with ice‑storm tree damage.
Assess property damage and recovery processes.
Evaluate preparedness and response capacity.
Identify strategies that strengthen future resilience.
Stage 1 priorities
  1. Framework to identify neighborhoods at risk of ice‑induced tree damage.
  2. Co‑designed interventions addressing community needs and priorities.
  3. Public education on urban trees and ice‑storm hazards.
